Introduction

Action cinema, characterized by intense scenes of violence, fights, chases, and armed conflict,
has been a popular genre among adolescents for decades. Since this stage of human development
is critical in shaping identity, behavior, and emotional structure, there is growing concern
about the real impact of such content. This research aims to analyze whether constant exposure
to action films significantly influences the psychological, emotional, or behavioral development
of adolescents, based on psychiatric and psychological evidence suggesting that such an impact does exist.

Statement

Exposure to violent audiovisual media has been the subject of numerous studies in psychology and psychiatry.
While in many cases it is argued that adolescents can differentiate fiction from reality, other studies
indicate that such exposure can alter their emotional responses, increase tolerance to violence, and even
influence interpersonal relationships and behavior. Therefore, the central question arises: To what extent
can action films disrupt the healthy development of adolescents?

Justification

Understanding the effect of action cinema on adolescent development is essential for parents, educators, and policymakers.
This research provides a critical review based on scientific evidence to determine whether this film genre is a factor that
negatively influences personality formation, conflict resolution, and empathy in young people.

Objectives

General Objective:
To determine whether frequent consumption of action films affects the psychological and emotional development of adolescents.

Specific Objectives:

Analyze psychiatric and psychological evidence on the effects of audiovisual violence on adolescent development.
Identify behaviors associated with excessive consumption of violent cinema.
Contrast scientific studies with popular perceptions on the topic.

Hypothesis

Main Hypothesis:

Regular consumption of action films negatively influences the emotional and behavioral development of adolescents.

Theoretical Framework

Various studies in psychiatry and neuroscience have shown that adolescents, being in a stage of neurological development,
are more sensitive to external stimuli. Psychiatrist Daniel Siegel argues that the prefrontal cortex
is still maturing during adolescence, which affects judgment and impulse control. Joseph LeDoux explains that repeated exposure
to violence can continuously activate the amygdala, leading to a prolonged state of alert.

Studies by Dr. Craig Anderson have linked constant exposure to violent media with increased aggression,
reduced empathy, and normalization of violence. The American Psychiatric Association (APA) has warned about
the risks of emotional desensitization in youth who regularly consume such content.
Additionally, research in developmental psychology suggests that contextual factors such as family and social environments
can modulate these effects, making it crucial to consider these variables in analysis.

Methodology

This study is based on a documentary review of scientific and medical sources specializing in developmental psychology,
child psychiatry, and neuroscience. More than ten peer-reviewed studies published between 2005 and 2023 were analyzed.
The research focuses on adolescents aged 12 to 18 who are regular consumers of action films.
Additionally, a comparative analysis of cases in different educational contexts was conducted to achieve data
triangulation that strengthens the validity and reliability of the results.

Analysis and Discussion

The review of studies suggests that while action films are not a direct cause of emotional or behavioral disorders,
they can act as a reinforcing factor for preexisting problematic behaviors or foster aggressive attitudes.
Adolescents frequently exposed to violent content show higher tolerance to aggression, lower empathetic capacity,
and in some cases, imitation of impulsive behaviors. It is important to note that, beyond these effects,
variables such as family environment, educational level, and social context play a fundamental role in how such behaviors manifest.
This implies that the impact can vary significantly from case to case, presenting a real risk for certain more vulnerable groups.

Conclusions

Action films can negatively affect the emotional and behavioral development of adolescents, especially when consumed excessively
and without proper guidance.
Scientific evidence supports the idea that repeated exposure to audiovisual violence contributes to emotional desensitization
and the normalization of aggression.
It is crucial for parents, guardians, and educators to promote a critical and moderate consumption of audiovisual content,
accompanied by dialogue and specific guidance to help mitigate potential negative effects.
It is recommended to implement mediation strategies and media literacy education in schools to counteract and reduce
adverse effects on youth development.
